A foundational property differentiating evolution from random search is mutational locality, the tendency for a mutant to resemble its parent \citep{maynardsmith1970natural,galvanlopez2011defining,ashlock2012representation,back1996evolutionary,foster2001evolutionary,richter2014fitness}.
This property hinges on the geometry of genotype space --- how all possible genetic sequences are arranged in mutational neighborhoods \citep{fragata2019evolution,castle2021towards}.
Although classic visualizations often paint genotype space in one or two dimensions \citep{mcleish2015there,greenbury2022structure,gordon1994evolution,gavrilets1997evolution}, it is more accurately conceptualized as a dense network where each genotype is surrounded by numerous single-mutant neighbors (Figure \ref{fig:fitness_landscapes}, left).

A second fundamental prerequisite for evolution by natural selection is variability in fitness between points in genotype space, which steer population trajectories --- creating a so-called ``fitness landscape'' (Figure \ref{fig:fitness_landscapes}, middle).
In scenarios where fitness values fluctuate (e.g., depending on ecological or environmental factors \citep{olson2014dynamic,kaplan2008end,dolson2021digital}), such a fitness landscape may instead be termed a``fitness seascape'' \citep{cairns2022strong,mustonen2009fitness}.
To decouple some complications of fitness volatility, another useful abstraction is the genotype-phenotype map \citep{ahnert2017structural,aguilar2018architecture,stadler2003landscapes}.
In biological systems, phenotype (observable traits) can often be more readily and consistently measured than fitness \citep{chen2022environmental,ahnert2017structural,wagner2011pleiotropic,aguilar2018architecture}.
In EC, the genotype-phenotype map abstraction encapsulates indirect encoding techniques, which can be used to help solve a problem by devising a (typically) lower-dimensional genetic \textit{representation} for it \citep{ashlock2012representation,rothlauf2003redundant,rothlauf2009representations,mitavskiy2003comparing}.
This approach essentially constructs a genotype space by defining a ``developmental'' mapping for translation into phenotype (i.e., solution) space \citep{stanley2007compositional,stanley2003taxonomy,stanley2009hypercubebased,clune2011performance,kitano1990designing,hornby2002creating}.

Though evolutionary adaptiation progresses most efficiently on landscapes that are monotonic and smooth, such conditions are rare in practice.
Instead, adaptive landscapes often exhibit \textit{rugedness} \citep{papkou2023rugged,reidys2001neutrality} and \textit{neutrality} (or near-neutrality) \citep{kimura1983neutral,kimura1991neutral,ohta1992nearly,ohta2002near}.
Even where evolutionary exploration is aided by large genome size \citep{franke2011evolutionary,greenbury2022structure}, drift effects \citep{reidys2001neutrality,ahnert2017structural,croitoru2022punctuated,galvanlopez2011neutrality,jorg2008neutral,huynen1996smoothness,shackleton2000investigation,huynen1996exploring,amitai2007latent,banzhaf2006evolution,hu2025neutrality,oliveto2017how,wagner2008robustness,wagner2013robustness,masel2010robustness,hu2018neutrality}, and far-reaching jumps via recombination \citep{li2024rapid,doerr2008crossover,fogel1994effectiveness} --- such properties can nonetheless meaningfully impede adaptation by restricting discovery of beneficial mutations \citep{martin2013multiple,steinberg2016environmental,li2025probability,bank2022epistasis,neidhart2014adaptation,orr2005genetic,bershtein2008advances,van1999neutral}.

As such, adaptive landscape theory has assumed a central role in the study of \textit{evolvability} --- defined broadly as the capability of a population to generate and maintain adaptive genetic variation \citep{payne2018causes,kirschner1998evolvability}.

Conditions where an adaptive landscape supplies ample mutational opportunity are described as having high evolvability, and vice versa.

Evolutionary computation and evolutionary biology have traditionally applied somewhat orthogonal perspectives to the problem of evolvability.
Evolutionary computation, where a population can be seeded entirely from scratch, often frames evolvability as a global property of how a given genotype-phenotype map organizes an adaptive landscape \citep{TODO}.
Because known biological life adheres to a common DNA/RNA sequence space \citep{aguirre2018networked}, such counterfactuals are more nebulous  \citep{zhang2015evolution}.
Instead, biological perspective frames evolvability as a local property of regions within a large genotype space \citep{wagner2017information,wagner2023evolvability}.

Across this gap, biological and computational notions of evolvability have already exerted reciporical influences \citep{kirschner1998evolvability}.
At root, nature proves evolvability can emerge undesigned, while artificial models makes clear it is by no means for granted.
In this spirit, we review work on evolvability across both field to highlight opportunities --- in both directions --- for interdisciplinary exchange.

\subsection{Evolvability in Evolutionary Computation}

Given the objective-oriented nature of evolutionary computation, interest in evolvability stems from problems with premature convergence, and guarding against it by balancing exploration against exploitation remains a persistent concern \citep{crepinsek2013exploration,andre2001improvement,bongard2010guarding,pandey2014comparative}.
While alternate strategies can help address these symptoms (e.g., metaheuristic analysis, parameter tuning, large-effect mutations, evolutionary restarts, etc.) \citep{TODOsuperexplorers,wang2018population,dang2016selfadaptation,fogel1994introduction,fouskakis2002stochastic,kirkpatrick1983optimization,de2015money,doerr2014impact}, most effort, however, has gone into \textit{designing} evolvable adaptive landscapes outright.

Numerous desirable properties of an evolvable representation have been highlighted \citep{moreno2017evolvability}:

A typical EC framing defines evolvability as the genotype space as containing viable and novel solutions \citep{tarapore2015evolvability}.
The \textit{evolvability signature}, for instance, profiles the distribution of phenotypic variation reachable from a genotype, enabling encodings to be compared beyond raw task performance \citep{tarapore2015evolvability}.

A particularly promising line of EC work has sprung up in investigating how to harness unsupervised learning (e.g., autoencoders, LLMs) to generate evolvable genotype-phenotype maps \citep{lehman2023evolution,moreno2018learning,bentley2022evolving,gaier2020discovering,wittenberg2023denoising}.
Under this framing, is considered as an embedding of a manifold within phenotype space.
While a number of possible frameworks exist, a popular notion has beenn that of the autoencoder where a lower-dimennsional representation can be automatically learned by piping an encoder and a decoder on training examples.

\subsection{Evolvability in Evolutionary Biology}

Biological genotype space is exponentially vast \citep{stadler2007fitness,de2014empirical}.
Even at just 20 sites, upwards of a quintillion mutational paths exist between any two strands of RNA \citep{greenbury2022structure}.
This vastness supplies many distinct local neighborhoods \citep{wagner2017information}.
For instance, in a 20-site strand, upwards of 2.59 million sequences exist such that all are at least 5 mutations apart; for 40, 400, and 4,000 site strands, the number of sequences at least 25\% mutually distinct grows as $2.03 \times 10^{11}$, $4.64 \times 10^{97}$, and $9.60 \times 10^{956}$ \citep{gilbert1952comparison,varshamov1957estimate}.
For comparison, on the order of $10^{88}$ photons have been estimated to exist in the observable universe \citep{dimopoulos1978baryon}.
Given these many distinct regions, opportunities where meaningful variability exists in local structural features of the landscape evolution can be exploited by evolution in interesting ways \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,steinberg2016environmental,gupta2022hostparasite,ferrare2024evolution,pyo2025evolutionary}.

Under such a framing, mutations can be considered ``evolvability enhancing'' if they provide access to later high fitness mutations, which are surprisingly abundant in biological fitness landscapes \citep{wagner2023evolvability} --- although the generality of this abundance remains to be explored \citep{ogbunugafor2023mutations}.
This process, however, requires selection to act on properties of a genome based on its broader neighborhood, in addition to its own fitness.
A key problem arises, thus, in that natural selection only operates on the current environment \citep{watson2016how}, whether it can allow optimization for long-term outcomes is heavily debated \citep{wagner2021adaptive,akay2016there,keller1999levels,altenberg1995genome,akay2016there}.
The idea that mutation and selection can select for better long-term outcomes has been conclusively shown in both biological and computational evolving systems \citep{sung2012driftbarrier, wilke2001evolution, pyo2025evolutionary, wagner2021adaptive, akay2016there, keller1999levels, wagner2017information, kumawat2024evolution, barnett2025experimental, steinberg2016environmental, hayden2011cryptic, gupta2022hostparasite, zaman2014coevolution}.

Another framing of this issue is that of lineage selection, an emerging view that, under certain conditions, evolution can act not just to optimize instantaneous fitness, but rather the long-term growth rate among competing lineages \citep{akay2016there,yoshimura1996evolution,yoshimura1991individual}.

Particular interest has arisen around the idea that changing environments can help exert lineage selection.
In a changing environment with varying requirement for survival \citep{bell2017evolutionary,bell2010fluctuating}, mutations may benefit a lineage directly, or with respect to an environment that is likely to appear in the future \citep{parter2008facilitated}.For instance, in a changing environment, a high-fitness genotype may be out-competed by genotypes that are lower in fitness, but belong to a lineage that switches quickly between phenotypes adapted to alternate environments \citep{woods2011secondorder}.
Recent work has shown that environmental change pushes populations to these boundaries where they can more freely mutate between alternate phenotypes \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,kussell2006polymerpopulation,ancel2005evolution}.
Systematic effort towards a taxonomy of second-order optimization protocols needs to be undertaken in future work \citep{gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability,hu2010evolvability}.

Biologists have developed several useful measures to quantify the ease with which evolution can respond to changing environment selection.
For example, researchers have shown that abundant edge-flips in a fitness landscape --- i.e., single mutation edges that change the direction of adaptation between two mutants in two environments --- can allow us to predict the efficacy of intermittent antimicrobial therapy \citep{chen2024inverted}.
Similarly, a measure called the mutation effect reaction norm (Mu-RN) has been used to quantify the effect of mutations across environments \citep{ogbunugafor2022mutation}.
Work on drug resistant mutations has also formalized the idea of \textit{deception} on fitness landscapes, which is the presence of epistatic mutations that lead populations away from global optima \citep{eppstein2016quantifying,ogbunugafor2016competition}.

Biological literature provides an abundance of structural properties that can be directly translated into the EC domain to motivate the design of solution spaces and algorithms that leverage second-order outcomes of the evolutionary process.
Much unsolved work remains in predicting under what conditions a population will localize to different boundary genotypes, \citep{kussell2006polymerpopulation}, which may be an opportunity for EC to contribute.

A key challenge for these two fields is to unite their approach, to think about how learned encodings can translate into movement within a space rather than shaping a space.
However, we believe that application-oriented genetic programming and genetic algorithms have special value to offer in the realm of genotype-phenotype maps and evolvability.
On account of connections to machine learning concepts, the question of generalization and double-descent are unique ideas that have ben led into biology from EC.
Corresponding work in biology, however, is only fledgling.
Indeed, such work considering evolvability as an unsupervised learning process has notably already been driven forward through collaboration with evolutionary computation practitioners \citep{kouvaris2017evolution,szilagyi2020phenotypes}.
Some authors have suggested that the evolutionary process can in some ways ``learn'' from previous environments to prevent overfitting \citep{kouvaris2017evolution, watson2016how, watson2014evolution}.
This theory may help to explain, as well as to attain more evolvable mappings.
Boundary localization generalizes to representation-based framings of evolvability \citep{reisinger2006selecting} --- although this may unfold on a slower (e.g., macro) evolutionary timescale \citep{reznick2009darwin}.
Thus, using large search spaces with a \textit{representation optimizing} algorithm can give better overall results than directly trying to find a good representation \citep{hewlett2008optimization,gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability}.
