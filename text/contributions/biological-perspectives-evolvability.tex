\label{sec:evolvability}

However, we believe that application-oriented genetic programming and genetic algorithms have special value to offer in the realm of genotype-phenotype maps and evolvability.
Owing to their artificial nature, genotype-phenotype maps --- much less, maps with strong evolvability --- do not come baked into evolutionary computation \textit{a priori} \citep{kirschner1998evolvability}.
As such, EC research has invested significant effort in teasing apart how the properties of a genotype-phenotype map influence outcomes from adaptive evolution \citep{banzhaf1994genotypephenotypemapping,hu2010evolvability,whigham2017mapping}.
A particularly promising line of EC work has sprung up in investigating how to harness unsupervised learning (e.g., autoencoders, LLMs) to generate evolvable genotype-phenotype maps \citep{lehman2023evolution,moreno2018learning,bentley2022evolving,gaier2020discovering,wittenberg2023denoising}.
Corresponding work in biology, however, is only fledgling.
Indeed, such work considering evolvability as an unsupervised learning process has notably already been driven forward through collaboration with evolutionary computation practitioners \citep{kouvaris2017evolution,szilagyi2020phenotypes}.
% Some authors have suggested that the evolutionary process can in some ways ``learn'' from previous environments to prevent overfitting \citep{kouvaris2017evolution, watson2016how, watson2014evolution}.

In a biological context, evolvability is defined as the capacity of a population to generate heritable phenotypic variation that facilitates adaptive evolution \citep{payne2018causes,kirschner1998evolvability}.
While a variety of mechanisms or facets have been brought to evolvability \citep{clune2008natural,hu2010evolvability,altenberg1994evolution,mengistu2016evolvability,riederer2022capturing}, it is typically thought of as related to the local structure of the fitness landscape defined with respect to available mutations \citep{wagner2017information,wagner2023evolvability}.
% If the fitness landscape contains many adjacent mutations that increase fitness, a population is more likely to adapt after one of these mutations appears.
If most adjacent mutations are deleterious, it severely limits the ability of the population to explore the genotype space \citep{orr2005genetic,bershtein2008advances}.
% A genotype with many deleterious mutations is essentially a local maximum in the fitness landscape and its presence can immobilize populations in specific genotypic states.
In evolutionary computation, this can contribute to the challlenge of \textit{premature convergence}, where a population reaches a local optimum but not a global optimum \citep{schmitt2003theory,andre2001improvement,bongard2010guarding,pandey2014comparative}.

Mutations can be considered ``evolvability enhancing'' if they provide access to later high fitness mutations, which are surprisingly abundant in biological fitness landscapes \citep{wagner2023evolvability} --- although the generality of this abundance remains to be explored \citep{ogbunugafor2023mutations}.
% An escalation in mutation rate may also enhance evolvability, but only if the fitness landscape has the potential to generate adaptive mutations \citep{kumawat2024evolution}.
% In most cases, mutation rates decrease rapidly to small values unless sustained by an environment that requires continual adaptation, in line with the \textit{drift barrier hypothesis} \citep{sung2012driftbarrier,clune2008natural,wei2022rapid}.

Although evolvability is critical to effective adaptations, traditional EC algorithms, however, do not directly prioritize evolvability \citep{tarapore2015evolvability}.
% Specifically in the context of EC, evolvability arises from a combination of many factors --- among the most direct ones being the structure of the solution space and the rate of mutation \citep{hu2010evolvability,altenberg1994evolution,mengistu2016evolvability}.
% As EC systems are engineered rather than evolved, the practitioner has some control over both of these facets.
% Here, a \textit{mutation operator} steps between solutions, sampling new candidate solutions from current ones and these alternate solutions are then probabilistically accepted based on the improvement in fitness that they provide \citep{foster2001evolutionary,back1996evolutionary}.
% In this way, the process of mutation and selection moves a population around the solution space in search of new and better solutions.
The structure of the space is strongly tied to the data structure used to encode the solutions and is generally referred to as a \textit{representation} \citep{ashlock2012representation,rothlauf2003redundant, rothlauf2009representations,mitavskiy2003comparing}, which together with the mutation operator controls how EC explores new solutions \citep{stark2004rate}.
% For example, a 1D optimization problem may be solved by either defining a 1D solution space and then allowing mutational jumps to far-flung solutions, or equivalently, by designing the space in a way that small changes in this representation correspond to large jumps on the domain of the problem.
% The problem of designing optimal representations is a core research field in EC \citep{ashlock2012representation, rothlauf2003redundant, rothlauf2009representations,mitavskiy2003comparing}.

Unlike EC where the user can design a suitable representation and mutation operator, in nature the ``representation'' for an evolving population is fixed to DNA/RNA sequence space composed of the four nucleotide bases \citep{aguirre2018networked}  (there is some potential to engineer it though, see \citet{zhang2015evolution}).
% There is little possibility for biological systems to manipulate this representation over microevolutionary time scales.
% Mutation rates are also subject to biophysical constraints, like the energetics of the DNA polymerase enzyme that replicates the genome \citep{loh2009optimization,fersht1981dna}.
However, within the exponentially vast space of genotypes \citep{stadler2007fitness,de2014empirical}, evolution can exploit local structural features of the landscape in interesting ways \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,steinberg2016environmental,gupta2022hostparasite,ferrare2024evolution,pyo2025evolutionary}.
% We discuss some of these findings here to motivate how they can guide research in evolutionary computation.

% When mutation rates are high, and adjacent mutations are deleterious, a population suffers a fitness penalty for every new mutation that appears \citep{anderson2004viral,lynch1993mutational}.
% In this case, there is a fitness advantage to lowering the mutation rate until the overall frequency of deleterious mutations is reduced, increasing the average fitness of the population \citep{sung2012driftbarrier}.
% On the other hand, if the mutation rate is fixed, populations adopt an alternate strategy where they evolve to locally ``flat'' areas of the fitness landscape where neighboring mutations have a similar fitness \citep{wilke2001evolution}.
% Here, a large fraction of substitutions that arise do not change fitness, preventing decreases in average population fitness due to the high mutation rate.
% Thus, instead of directly molding the fitness landscape, evolution is able to draw upon local structures in the genotype space to find areas where the distribution of the fitness effects (DFE) of mutations is less negative \citep{eyrewalker2007distribution}.
% If mutation rates can be regulated, decreasing the mutation rate as fitness improves can also aid convergence to a fitness peak and subsequently reduce the propensity to create deleterious mutants once high fitness is achieved \citep{pyo2025evolutionary}.
% This decrease in mutation rate mimics the ``cooling schedule'' seen in stochastic optimization algorithms like simulated annealing \citep{kirkpatrick1983optimization}.
% EC algorithms generally start out with very high mutation rates compared to biological populations and frequently require such cooling schedules for optimal convergence.

In a changing environment with varying requirement for survival \citep{bell2017evolutionary,bell2010fluctuating}, mutations may benefit a lineage directly, or with respect to an environment that is likely to appear in the future \citep{parter2008facilitated}.
In the context of EC, contradictory objectives associated with changing environments can be associated to multi-objective optimization where using a fluctuating selection procedure might even help the algorithm converge to far away optima that were inaccessible to the optimization process in a static environment \citep{steinberg2016environmental}.
% Here, modifying the hyperparameters such that a localization on fitness landscape boundaries can manifest may help find a part of the solution space with a structure optimized for a fluctuating objective \citep{kussell2006polymerpopulation, kumawat2024evolution}.
% An even stronger advantage emerges if switching between alternate solutions that are individually optimal under distinct objectives is preferred over choosing a generalist solution.
Because natural selection only operates on the current environment \citep{watson2016how}, whether it can allow optimization for long-term outcomes is heavily debated \citep{wagner2021adaptive, akay2016there, keller1999levels, altenberg1995genome}.
Regardless, some areas of genome space are likely to have different phenotypes available locally \citep{wagner2017information},
% if the fitness landscape is large and varied, as most biological landscapes are, it is likely to contain genotypes that lie between mutants having phenotypes that are adapted to different environments \citep{wagner2017information}.
% These phenotypic ``boundaries'' in the genotype space can in turn act as hotspots for evolvability in a changing environment.
and recent work has shown that environmental change pushes populations to these boundaries where they can more freely mutate between alternate phenotypes \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental}.
(redicting under what conditions a population will localize to different boundary genotypes, however, remains unsolved \citep{kussell2006polymerpopulation}.
% \textit{Generalist} genotypes, with a phenotype adapted to multiple environments, are obviously better than evolvable ones, but are unlikely to exist if the alternate environments are characterized by an inherent \textit{fitness tradeoff}.
% For example, if becoming resistant to an antibiotic requires the expression of a protein that is toxic for the cell, this resistant cell will be out-competed by sensitive cells in an environment where the antibiotic is absent.

% Apart from localization on such phenotypic boundaries, a changing environment also provides opportunities for discovery of new variation by directly shaping population dynamics \citep{peniston2020environmental, steinberg2016environmental,devos2015breaking}.
% Environmental change induced deformations in the fitness landscape can push the population away from genotypes that are locally fit in a specific environment, which in turn allows the population to sample new genotypes in the landscape \citep{steinberg2016environmental}.
% Even neutral evolution --- driven by random mutations and drift in a subset of the mutational space --- can sometimes help relax the immobilization of populations on fitness peaks \citep{hayden2011cryptic}.
% While recent EC works have started exploring this area, a substantial push to understand how neutrality affects optimization in EC problems is still lacking \citep{galvanlopez2011neutrality}.
% Like environmental change, the presence of parasites can also lead to fitness deformations and anomalous population dynamics, albeit with some direct feedback on the deforming process (See \ref{sec:coevolution}).

These second order effects, which run counter to the common adage of ``survival of the fittest,'' have received significant attention \citep{akay2016there} --- with an emerging view that, under certain conditions, evolution can act not just to optimize instantaneous fitness, but rather the long-term growth rate among competing lineages \citep{akay2016there,yoshimura1996evolution,yoshimura1991individual}.
For instance, in a changing environment, a high fitness genotype may be out-competed by genotypes that are lower in fitness, but belong to a lineage that switches quickly between phenotypes adapted to alternate environments \citep{woods2011secondorder}.
The idea that mutation and selection can select for better long term outcomes has been conclusively shown in both biological and computational evolving systems \citep{sung2012driftbarrier, wilke2001evolution, pyo2025evolutionary, wagner2021adaptive, akay2016there, keller1999levels, wagner2017information, kumawat2024evolution, barnett2025experimental, steinberg2016environmental, hayden2011cryptic, gupta2022hostparasite, zaman2014coevolution}.
% These biological results illuminate several promising directions for research in evolutionary computation.

% While EC has a large repertoire of algorithms and selection schemes designed to reach high fitness solutions, we lack a clear understanding of how these algorithms shape the local search space \citep{crepinsek2013exploration}.
% For example, can these algorithms select for optimal mutation rates if mutation rates are allowed to evolve?
% Do they lead populations to smooth or rugged parts of the solution space?
It is plausible that certain selection schemes used regularly in EC are already good at optimizing lineage-level aggregate fitness, and at converging to high fitness solutions \citep{hu2010evolvability,doncieux2020novelty,lehman2015enhancing}.
% Studying these second-order effects for different EC algorithms can be a promising direction for future work.

Boundary localization generalizes to representation-based framings of evolvability \citep{reisinger2006selecting} --- although this may unfold on a slower (e.g., macro) evolutionary timescale \citep{reznick2009darwin}.
Thus, using large search spaces with a \textit{representation optimizing} algorithm can give better overall results than directly trying to find a good representation \citep{hewlett2008optimization,gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability}.
% Whether we can find algorithms that favor second order properties remains to be seen.
Systematic effort towards a taxonomy of second-order optimization protocols needs to be undertaken in future work \citep{gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability,hu2010evolvability}.
The above discussion on biological outcomes provides some hints to guide their design.
