\section{Biological Perspectives on Evolvability}
\label{sec:evolvability}

In a biological context, evolvability is defined as the capacity of a population to generate heritable phenotypic variation that facilitates adaptive evolution \citep{payne2019causes,kirschner1998evolvability}. Evolvability has various mechanisms or facets---ranging from changes in the variation that is generated during evolution, to changes to the process of selection itself \citep{riederer2022capturing}. At a fundamental level, the local structure of the fitness landscape (defined with respect to the genotypes present in the population) influences the evolvability of a population \citep{wagner2017information,wagner2023evolvabilityenhancing}. If the fitness landscape contains many adjacent mutations that increase fitness, a population is more likely to adapt after one of these mutations appears. However, if most adjacent mutations are deleterious, it severely limits the ability of the population to explore the genotype space, hindering long-term adaptation and phenotypic innovation \citep{orr2005genetic,bershtein2008advances}. A genotype with many deleterious mutations is essentially a local maximum in the fitness landscape and its presence can immobilize populations in specific genotypic states. This phenomenon where a process achieves a suboptimal solution at a local optimum and fails to explore new candidate solutions is referred to as \textit{premature convergence} in the field of optimization, and has been a central challenge in the design of  EC algorithms \citep{schmitt2003theory,andre2001improvement,bongard2010guarding,pandey2014comparative}.  Alternative to mutations that lead to such local optima, one may define certain mutations as ``evolvability enhancing'' if they provide access to high fitness mutations but are themselves not proportionally fitter compared to the reference genotype. These mutations have been found to be surprisingly abundant in biological fitness landscapes, allowing easy adaptation towards novel phenotypes \citep{wagner2023evolvabilityenhancing}. Whether this abundance is an inherent feature of the selection process or a special property of these evolutionary substrates remains to be explored \citep{ogbunugafor2023mutations}. An escalation in mutation rate may also enhance evolvability, but only if the fitness landscape has the potential to generate adaptive mutations \citep{kumawat2025evolution}. In most cases, mutation rates decrease rapidly to small values unless sustained by an environment that requires continual adaptation, in line with the \textit{drift barrier hypothesis} \citep{sung2012driftbarrier,clune2008natural,wei2022rapid}.  

As evident from the biological literature and the work on premature convergence, evolvability allows populations to find new phenotypes and adaptations rapidly and is thus an extremely desirable feature. Traditional EC algorithms, however,  do not prioritize evolvability as an important target \citep{tarapore2015evolvability}. Specifically in the context of EC, evolvability arises from a combination of many factors --- among the most direct ones being the structure of the solution space and the rate of mutation \citep{hu2010evolvability,altenberg1994evolution,mengistu2016evolvability,altenberg1994evolution}. As EC systems are engineered rather than evolved, the practitioner has some control over both of these facets. Here, a \textit{mutation operator} steps between solutions, sampling new candidate solutions from current ones and these alternate solutions are then probabilistically accepted based on the improvement in fitness that they provide \citep{foster2001evolutionary,back1996evolutionary}. In this way, the process of mutation and selection moves a population around the solution space in search of new and better solutions. The structure of the space is strongly tied to the data structure used to encode the solutions and is generally referred to as a \textit{representation} \citep{ashlock2012representation}. The mutation operator and the representation together determine the rate at which an EC algorithm explores new solutions \citep{stark2004rate}. For example, a 1D optimization problem may be solved by either defining a 1D solution space and then allowing mutational jumps to far-flung solutions, or equivalently, by designing the space in a way that small changes in this representation correspond to large jumps on the domain of the problem. The problem of designing optimal representations is a core research field in EC \citep{ashlock2012representation, rothlauf2003redundant, rothlauf2009representations,mitavskiy2003comparing}.

Unlike EC where the user can design a suitable representation and mutation operator, in nature the ``representation'' for an evolving population is fixed---it is the DNA/RNA sequence space composed of the four nucleotide bases \citep{aguirre2018networked}. There is little possibility for biological systems to manipulate this representation over microevolutionary time scales (there is some potential to engineer it though, see \citet{zhang2015evolution}). Mutation rates are also subject to biophysical constraints, like the energetics of the DNA polymerase enzyme that replicates the genome \citep{loh2010optimization,fersht1981dna}. Despite these limitations, the large length of DNA genomes means that the genotype space and the resulting fitness landscape also contain a vast number of genotypes, a number that scales exponentially with length \citep{stadler2007fitness,de2014empirical}. Thus, while the representation problem is technically constrained, a possibility for local modifications to the fitness landscape still exists because of this exceptionally large set of states that a genotype can take, each with a different local structure. Indeed, recent work on fitness landscapes has shown that even without a process directly shaping the fitness landscape (the biological representation), evolution can exploit local structural features of the landscape in interesting ways \citep{kumawat2025evolution,barnett2025experimental,steinberg2016environmental,gupta2022hostparasite,ferrare2024evolution,pyo2025evolutionary}. We discuss some of these findings here to motivate how they can guide research in evolutionary computation.

When mutation rates are high, and adjacent mutations are deleterious, a population suffers a fitness penalty for every new mutation that appears \citep{anderson2004viral,lynch1993mutational}. In this case, there is a fitness advantage to lowering the mutation rate until the overall frequency of deleterious mutations is reduced, increasing the average fitness of the population \citep{sung2012driftbarrier}. On the other hand, if the mutation rate is fixed, populations adopt an alternate strategy where they evolve to locally ``flat'' areas of the fitness landscape where neighboring mutations have a similar fitness \citep{wilke2001evolution}. Here, a large fraction of substitutions that arise do not change fitness, preventing decreases in average population fitness due to the high mutation rate. Thus, instead of directly molding the fitness landscape, evolution is able to draw upon local structures in the genotype space to find areas where the distribution of the fitness effects (DFE) of mutations is less negative \citep{eyrewalker2007distribution}. If mutation rates can be regulated, decreasing the mutation rate as fitness improves can also aid convergence to a fitness peak and subsequently reduce the propensity to create deleterious mutants once high fitness is achieved \citep{pyo2025evolutionary}. This decrease in mutation rate mimics the ``cooling schedule'' seen in stochastic optimization algorithms like simulated annealing \citep{kirkpatrick1983optimization}. EC algorithms generally start out with very high mutation rates compared to biological populations and frequently require such cooling schedules for optimal convergence.

In a changing environment, the requirement for survival is altered \citep{bell2017evolutionary,bell2010fluctuating}. Here, the population must continually acquire mutations that are not just adaptive, but adaptive in an environment that is likely to appear in the future \citep{parter2008facilitated}. The process of natural selection, however, is myopic---i.e., it drives an increase in the abundance of mutations that are fit in the current environment, without regards to the fitness of these mutations in the future \citep{watson2016can}. Whether it can allow optimization for long-term outcomes is heavily debated \citep{wagner2022adaptive, akcay2016there, keller1999levels, altenberg1995genome}. Some authors have suggested that the evolutionary process can in some ways ``learn'' from previous environments to prevent overfitting \citep{kouvaris2017evolution, watson2016can, watson2014evolution}. Regardless, if the fitness landscape is large and varied, as most biological landscapes are, it is likely to contain genotypes that lie between mutants having phenotypes that are adapted to different environments \citep{wagner2017information}. These phenotypic ``boundaries'' in the genotype space can in turn act as hotspots for evolvability in a changing environment. Recent work has shown that environmental change can in fact push populations to these boundaries under certain modes of environmental change, allowing them to generate mutations to distinct phenotypes adapted to two alternative environments much more rapidly than they would at an adaptive peak in the landscape \citep{kumawat2025evolution,barnett2025experimental}. The exact evolutionary parameters that allow this ``localization'' on boundary genotypes, and predicting the location of these genotypes based on fitness landscape data is a problem that is yet to be resolved \cite{kussell2006polymerpopulation}. \textit{Generalist} genotypes, with a phenotype adapted to multiple environments, are obviously better than evolvable ones, but are unlikely to exist if the alternate environments are characterized by an inherent \textit{fitness tradeoff}. For example, if becoming resistant to an antibiotic requires the expression of a protein that is toxic for the cell, this resistant cell will be out-competed by sensitive cells in an environment where the antibiotic is absent. 

Apart from localization on such phenotypic boundaries, a changing environment also provides opportunities for discovery of new variation by directly shaping population dynamics \citep{peniston2020environmental, steinberg2016environmental,devos2015breaking}. Environmental change induced deformations in the fitness landscape can push the population away from genotypes that are locally fit in a specific environment, which in turn allows the population to sample new genotypes in the landscape \citep{steinberg2016environmental}. Even neutral evolution---driven by random mutations and drift in a subset of the mutational space---can sometimes help relax the immobilization of populations on fitness peaks \citep{hayden2011cryptic}. While recent EC works have started exploring this area, a substantial push to understand how neutrality affects optimization in EC problems is still lacking \citep{galvanlopez2011neutrality}. Like environmental change, the presence of parasites can also lead to fitness deformations and anomalous population dynamics, albeit with some direct feedback on the deforming process (See \ref{sec:coevolution}). Parasite populations suppress fitness for genotypes in large sections of the fitness landscape, making the host populations adapt towards new alternate genotypes \citep{gupta2022hostparasite,zaman2014coevolution,kussell2006polymerpopulation, kumawat2021architecture}. This host-parasite feedback has also been shown to aid evolvability in some cases, leading to the emergence of hosts with a genotype that changes phenotype within a single point mutation, and has a larger number of such point mutations that do change phenotype \cite{zaman2014coevolution}.

These second order effects, which run counter to the common adage of ``survival of the fittest'', have received significant attention \citep{akcay2016there}. The emerging view is that the process of evolution by natural selection, under certain conditions, acts not to optimize instantaneous fitness, but rather the long-term growth rate among competing lineages---i.e., the collective probability of survival of a genotype and its descendants \citep{akcay2016there,yoshimura1996evolution,yoshimura1991individual}. Thus, in a changing environment, a high fitness genotype may be out-competed by genotypes that are lower in fitness, but belong to a lineage that switches quickly between phenotypes adapted to alternate environments \citep{woods2011secondorder}. The idea that mutation and selection can select for better long term outcomes has been conclusively shown in both real and computational evolving systems \citep{sung2012driftbarrier, wilke2001evolution, pyo2025evolutionary, wagner2022adaptive, akcay2016there, keller1999levels, wagner2017information, kumawat2025evolution, barnett2025experimental, steinberg2016environmental, hayden2011cryptic, gupta2022hostparasite, zaman2014coevolution}. These biological results illuminate several promising directions for research in evolutionary computation.

While EC has a large repertoire of algorithms and selection schemes designed to reach high fitness solutions, we lack a clear understanding of how these algorithms shape the local search space \citep{crepinsek2013exploration}. For example, can these algorithms select for optimal mutation rates if mutation rates are allowed to evolve? Do they lead populations to smooth or rugged parts of the solution space? It is plausible that certain selection schemes used regularly in EC are already good at optimizing lineage-level aggregate fitness, and at converging to high fitness solutions \citep{hu2010evolvability,doncieux2020novelty,lehman2015enhancing}. Studying these second-order effects for different EC algorithms can be a promising direction for future work. Biological work also indicates that if the solution space is large and heterogeneous, the problem of finding alternate representations may be substituted with the problem of finding parts of the fitness landscape that can stand-in for an optimized representation \citep{reisinger2006selecting}. Thus, using large search spaces with a \textit{representation optimizing} algorithm can give better overall results than directly trying to find a good representation \citep{hewlett2008optimization,gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability}. Whether we can find algorithms that favor second order properties remains to be seen. While some metrics and signatures have been proposed to identify such algorithms, a systematic effort towards a taxonomy of second-order optimization protocols needs to be undertaken in future work \citep{gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability,hu2010evolvability}. The above discussion on biological outcomes provides some hints to guide their design. It is also important to note that the evolutionary optimization of these second-order features is likely to be slower, in some ways mirroring the micro/macro-evolutionary divide seen in biological evolution \citep{reznick2009darwin}. Like regular utility optimization problems, it might be necessary to instantiate the process with multiple random initial conditions rather than expecting the algorithm to converge to a globally optimal region of the fitness landscape \citep{de2015money, doerr2014impact}.

Here, modifying the hyperparameters such that a localization on fitness landscape boundaries can manifest may help find a part of the solution space with a structure optimized for a fluctuating objective \citep{kussell2006polymerpopulation, kumawat2025evolution}. An even stronger advantage emerges if switching between alternate solutions that are individually optimal under distinct objectives is preferred over choosing a generalist solution. Using a fluctuating selection procedure might even help the algorithm converge to far away optima that were inaccessible to the optimization process in a static environment \citep{steinberg2016environmental}. Finally, the representation itself determines the solution candidates that neighbor each other in the search space. Apart from leading to better solutions, certain representations may provide a better substrate for an evolutionary algorithm to find optimal local neighborhoods where these second order effects can manifest.
