A foundational property differentiating evolution from random search is mutational locality, the tendency for a mutant to resemble its parent \citep{maynardsmith1970natural,galvanlopez2011defining,ashlock2012representation,back1996evolutionary,foster2001evolutionary,richter2014fitness}.
This property hinges on the geometry of genotype space --- how all possible genetic sequences are arranged in mutational neighborhoods \citep{fragata2019evolution,castle2021towards}.
Although visual analogies have clasically painted genotype space in one or two dimensions \citep{mcleish2015there,greenbury2022structure,gordon1994evolution,gavrilets1997evolution}, it is more accurately conceptualized as a dense network where each genotype is surrounded by numerous single-mutant neighbors (Figure \ref{fig:fitness_landscapes}, left).

A second fundamental prerequisite for evolution by natural selection is variability in fitness between points in genotype space, creating a so-called ``fitness landscape'' (Figure \ref{fig:fitness_landscapes}, middle).
In scenarios where fitness values fluctuate (e.g., depending on ecological or environmental factors \citep{olson2014dynamic,kaplan2008end,dolson2021digital}), such a fitness landscape may instead be termed a``fitness seascape'' \citep{cairns2022strong,mustonen2009fitness}.
To decouple some complications of fitness volatility, another useful abstraction is the genotype-phenotype map \citep{ahnert2017structural,aguilar2018architecture,stadler2003landscapes}.
In biological systems, the phenotype (observable traits) can often be more readily and consistently measured than fitness \citep{chen2022environmental,ahnert2017structural,wagner2011pleiotropic,aguilar2018architecture}.
In EC, work around the genotype-phenotype map abstraction encapsulates indirect encoding techniques.
These methods can help solve a problem by devising a (typically) lower-dimensional genetic \textit{representation} for it \citep{ashlock2012representation,rothlauf2003redundant,rothlauf2009representations,mitavskiy2003comparing}.
In practice,  such a construction operates by defining a ``developmental'' mapping from genotype space into phenotype (i.e., solution) space \citep{stanley2007compositional,stanley2003taxonomy,stanley2009hypercubebased,clune2011performance,kitano1990designing,hornby2002creating}.

Though evolutionary adaptiation progresses most efficiently on landscapes that are monotonic and smooth, such conditions occur rarely.
Instead, adaptive landscapes often exhibit \textit{rugedness} \citep{papkou2023rugged,reidys2001neutrality} and \textit{neutrality} (or near-neutrality) \citep{kimura1983neutral,kimura1991neutral,ohta1992nearly,ohta2002near}.
Even where evolutionary exploration is aided by the mutational dimensionality large genomes \citep{franke2011evolutionary,greenbury2022structure}, drift effects \citep{reidys2001neutrality,ahnert2017structural,croitoru2022punctuated,galvanlopez2011neutrality,jorg2008neutral,huynen1996smoothness,shackleton2000investigation,huynen1996exploring,amitai2007latent,banzhaf2006evolution,hu2025neutrality,oliveto2017how,wagner2008robustness,wagner2013robustness,masel2010robustness,hu2018neutrality}, and far-reaching jumps via recombination \citep{li2024rapid,doerr2008crossover,fogel1994effectiveness} --- such properties can nonetheless meaningfully impede adaptation by restricting discovery of beneficial mutations \citep{martin2013multiple,steinberg2016environmental,li2025probability,bank2022epistasis,neidhart2014adaptation,orr2005genetic,bershtein2008advances,van1999neutral}.

Given its strong influence on mutational supply, adaptive landscape analyses have assumed a central role in the study of \textit{evolvability} --- defined broadly as the capability of a population to produce adaptive genetic variation \citep{payne2018causes,kirschner1998evolvability,wilder2015reconciling,pigliucci2008evolvability}.
\unskip\footnote{%
In addition to local adaptive landscape topology, within-population heterogeneity can also factor in to discussions of evolvability \citep{wilder2015reconciling,houle1992comparing}.
}
Conditions where an adaptive landscape supplies ample mutational opportunity are described as having ``high evolvability,'' and vice versa.

Orthogonal perspectives are brought to evolvability by evolutionary computation and evolutionary biology.
In evolutionary computation, where alternate genotype-phenotype maps freely originate, it makes sense to consider evolvability as a global property a given representation \citep{TODO}.
However, because known life adheres to a common DNA/RNA representation \citep{aguirre2018networked}, such counterfactuals are more limited in biology \citep{zhang2015evolution}.
Instead, over the course of hundreds of millions of years, natural history has traversed vast portions of genetic sequence space.
Thus, to evolutionary biologists, evolvability is more naturally framed as a local property of regions within a large genotype space \citep{wagner2017information,wagner2023evolvability}.

On account of these alternate formulations, biological and computational perspectives on evolvability have exerted strong reciporical influences \citep{kirschner1998evolvability,dawkins1989evolution,nuodelarosa2017computing}.
In this spirit, we review work on evolvability across both field to highlight opportunities --- in both directions --- for interdisciplinary exchange.

\subsection{Evolvability in Evolutionary Computation}

Given the objective-oriented nature of EC, much of interest in evolvability revolves around problems where evolution gets stuck \citep{crepinsek2013exploration,andre2001improvement,bongard2010guarding,pandey2014comparative,squillero2016divergence}.
In addition to numerous \textit{ad hoc} remedies (e.g., metaheuristic analysis, parameter tuning, large-effect mutations, evolutionary restarts, etc.) \citep{ragusa2022augmenting,wang2018population,dang2016selfadaptation,fogel1994introduction,fouskakis2002stochastic,kirkpatrick1983optimization,de2015money,doerr2014impact}, a common strategy to address such premaure convergence is to rearrange search space.

Framed around capability for continued evolutionary progress, \citet{tarapore2015evolvability} define evolvability as capability to produce variation that is both (1) viable and (2) novel.
These properties can be tested using an \textit{evolvability signature} assay, which samples outcomes produced under mutation to assess how strongly fitness constraints penalize phenotypic change.

At a mechanistic level, numerous desirable properties of an ``evolvable'' representation have been proposed \citep{moreno2017evolvability}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textit{modularity}, mechanistic decoupling between phenotypic traits that allows them to vary independently \citep{kashtan2005spontaneous,kashtan2007varying,wilder2015reconciling,owens2003quest};
\item \textit{pleiotropy}, the capability for mutation to affect coordinated change across multiple phenotypic traits \citep{rimbault2013derived,kirschner1998evolvability,cheney2014unshackling};
\item \textit{degeneracy}, overlapping function among structurally distinct elements which can enhance opportunity for variation \citep{whitacre2010degeneracy,wagner2003evolutionary};
\item \textit{canalization}, buffering of phenotypic traits against environmental and genetic perturbation (e.g., self-repair, compensatory balancing) \citep{gibson2004uncovering,paaby2014cryptic};
\item \textit{anisotropy}, biased over-representation certain phenotypes in genotype space \citep{schaper2014arrival,borenstein2008end,brunusan2025minimal,rohner2025macroevolution,herreralvarez2025structure}; and
\item \textit{symmmetry}, bias specifically toward phenotypic structure exhibiting regularity and repition \citep{coyne1987lack,clune2011performance,tonelli2013relationships,cheney2014unshackling}.
\end{itemize}

A particularly promising line of EC work has sprung up in investigating how to harness unsupervised learning (e.g., autoencoders, LLMs) to generate evolvable genotype-phenotype maps \citep{lehman2023evolution,moreno2018learning,bentley2022evolving,gaier2020discovering,wittenberg2023denoising,bontrager2018deepmasterprints,rodrigueztorrado2020bootstrapping,volz2018evolving,rakicevic2021policy,thakkar2019autoencoder,yosinski2012visually,feng2017autoencoding,stuurman2023approach,li2024generating,li2024generating,song2024morphvae}.
Under this framing, evolutionary history is considered as an unsupervised training data set.
Importantly, the set of possible ancestral phenotypes represent only a small fraction of phenotype space --- the vast majority of which, typically, is of low viability.
One terminology for this is that viable phenotypes might occupy a manifold, or lower-dimensional embedding, within phenotype space \citep{tenenbaum2000global,roweis2000nonlinear}.

Unsupervised learning approaches allow patterns of such manifolds within a larger space to be learned, and can facilitate sampling from that manifold; for instance, images of human faces represent a very small manifold within the larger space of all possible images, and it is possible to use example images of faces to learn this manifold and sample from it \citep{radford2015unsupervised,turk1991eigenface,white2016sampling,frowd2004evofit}.
Thus, an unsupervised learning approach over the set of viable phenotypes can produce a representation that aids in exploring and generating viable phenotypes.
This can be accomplished by reducing the dimensionality of genotype space (e.g., bottleneck autoencoder or generative adversarial network) and/or by repairing inviable phenotypes to map them back onto surface of the manifold (e.g., denoising autoencoder).
Learned mutational operators may also be possible \citep{lehman2023evolution,meyerson2024language}.

\subsection{Evolvability in Evolutionary Biology}

Biological genotype space is exponentially vast \citep{stadler2007fitness,de2014empirical}.
Even at just 20 sites, upwards of a quintillion mutational paths exist between any two strands of RNA \citep{greenbury2022structure}.
This vastness supplies many distinct local neighborhoods \citep{wagner2017information}.
For instance, in a 20-site strand, upwards of 2.59 million sequences exist such that all are at least 5 mutations apart; for 40, 400, and 4,000 site strands, the number of sequences at least 25\% mutually distinct grows as $2.03 \times 10^{11}$, $4.64 \times 10^{97}$, and $9.60 \times 10^{956}$ \citep{gilbert1952comparison,varshamov1957estimate}.
For comparison, on the order of $10^{88}$ photons have been estimated to exist in the observable universe \citep{dimopoulos1978baryon}.
Given these many distinct regions, opportunities where meaningful variability exists in local structural features of the landscape evolution can be exploited by evolution in interesting ways \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,steinberg2016environmental,gupta2022hostparasite,ferrare2024evolution,pyo2025evolutionary}.

Under a localized framing of evolvability, mutations can be considered ``evolvability enhancing'' if they shift into a region of genome space that provides access to later high fitness mutations.
Such mutations are surprisingly abundant in biological fitness landscapes \citep{wagner2023evolvability} --- although the generality of this abundance remains to be explored \citep{ogbunugafor2023mutations}.

Localization to evolvable regions, however, requires selection to act on properties of a genome based on its broader neighborhood, in addition to its own fitness.
Because natural selection only operates on the current environment \citep{watson2016how}, whether it can allow optimization for long-term outcomes is heavily debated \citep{wagner2021adaptive,akay2016there,keller1999levels,altenberg1995genome,yoshimura1996evolution,yoshimura1991individual,pigliucci2008evolvability}.
Such lineage selection effects may operate at various scales, even being postulated to act in a macroevolutionary fashion on species \citep{TODO}.
In certain scenarios, such selection for long-term outcomes has indeed been conclusively demonstrated in both biological and computational evolving systems \citep{sung2012driftbarrier,wilke2001evolution,pyo2025evolutionary,wagner2021adaptive,akay2016there,keller1999levels,wagner2017information,kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,steinberg2016environmental,hayden2011cryptic,gupta2022hostparasite,zaman2014coevolution}.

Particular interest has arisen around the idea that changing environments can help exert lineage selection.
In a changing environment with varying requirement for survival \citep{bell2017evolutionary,bell2010fluctuating}, mutations may benefit a lineage directly, or with respect to an environment that is likely to appear in the future \citep{parter2008facilitated}.
For instance, in a changing environment, a high-fitness genotype may be out-competed by genotypes that are lower in fitness, but belong to a lineage that switches quickly between phenotypes adapted to alternate environments \citep{woods2011secondorder}.
Recent work has shown that environmental change pushes populations to these boundaries where they can more freely mutate between alternate phenotypes \citep{kumawat2024evolution,barnett2025experimental,kussell2006polymerpopulation,ancel2005evolution}.

Biologists have developed several useful measures to quantify the how challenging it is to co-optimize on multiple adaptive landscapes.
Such a measure allows prediction of how readily a population adapting under one environment can continue to adapt once the environment shfits, and hence of how therapeutic strategies might be designed to steer or stall that adaptation.
For example, researchers have shown that abundant edge-flips in a fitness landscape --- i.e., single mutation edges that change the direction of adaptation between two mutants in two environments --- can allow us to predict the efficacy of intermittent antimicrobial therapy \citep{chen2024inverted}.
Similarly, a measure called the mutation effect reaction norm (Mu-RN) has been used to quantify the effect of mutations across environments \citep{ogbunugafor2022mutation}.
With respect to evolvability, such measures may predict when fluctuating selection may favor flexibility over local fitness.

Work on drug resistant mutations has also formalized the idea of \textit{deception} on fitness landscapes, which is the presence of epistatic mutations that lead populations away from global optima \citep{eppstein2016quantifying,ogbunugafor2016competition}.
These are quantified measuring the fraction of individuals possessing misleading mutation combinations --- combinations providing higer-than-average fitness, even though they lead away from the global optimum.
The measure may also be weighted by the magnitude of misleading mutations' adaptive advantage.

\subsection{Interdisciplinary Exchanges on Evolvability}

More broadly, natural history proves sophisticated evolvability can emerge undesigned, while challenges encountered by artificial models makes clear such evolvability is by no means for granted.

Localized conceptions of evolvability have been explored in evolutionary computation \citep{mengistu2016evolvability,doncieux2020novelty,katona2021quality,lehman2016critical,lehman2013evolvability,reisinger2007acquiring}.
The concept has been framed as a distinction between the inherent evolvability of a representation, and the acquired evolvability, which is related to a specific position in the search space \citep{reisinger2005towards}.
In fact, notable success has had in directly selecting for evolvability \citep{mengistu2016evolvability}, but the second-order processes such as changing fitness landscapes that drive it are less thoroughly characterized in EC \citep{grefenstette1999evolvability,richter2009detecting}.
Biological literature provides insight into how second-order outcomes of the evolutionary process might shape evolvability, as well as an abundance of structural properties that can be directly translated into the EC domain.
Much unsolved work remains in predicting under what conditions a population will localize to different boundary genotypes \citep{kussell2006polymerpopulation}, however, and so there is also an opportunity for EC to contribute in developing this area of knowledge.
In fact, it is plausible that certain selection schemes used regularly in EC already exhibit second-order optimization lineage-level aggregate fitness \citep{hu2010evolvability,doncieux2020novelty,lehman2015enhancing}.
However, systematic effort towards a taxonomy of second-order optimization protocols needs to be undertaken in future work \citep{gajewski2019evolvability,mengistu2016evolvability,hu2010evolvability}.
