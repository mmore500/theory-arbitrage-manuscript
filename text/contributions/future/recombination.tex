\subsection{Recombination}
\label{sec:recombination}

The advantage that recombination confers for adaptation has long been recognized in both evolutionary biology and evolutionary computation \citep{felsenstein1976evolutionary,holland1975adaptation}.
In addition to crossover, biology also exhibits widespread horizontal gene transfer (HGT) between lineages (i.e., one-way via conjugation between neighboring cells, transduction via viral intermediaries, and transformation by uptake of environmental DNA) \citep{arnold2022horizontal}.
These processes have profoundly shaped, in particular, the evolutionary trajectories of microorganisms, extending regulatory networks \citep{price2008horizontal}, modifying metabolic pathways \citep{goyal2022horizontal}, and promoting modularity \citep{kreimer2008evolution} --- through transfers of individual genes, or in  clusters of up to 40 genes \citep{brinkmann2018horizontal,martin2018physiological}.
Despite its potential to benefit the constructive power of evolution \citep{wiser2019horizontal}, HGT has been explored but not yet gained widespread adoption in evolutionary computation \citep{goldbarg2009transgeneic,peralesgravan2008am,harvey2011microbial,mehrafsa2013high,tomko2013unconstrain,atkinson2020horizontal}.

Both of these phenomena create phylogenetic networks --- phylogeny structures that depart from a strict trie of lineage splitting to also have events where lineages combine or interact.
In explorations of this topic, one important consideration will be being able to characterize the extent and pervasiveness and influence of horizontal gene transfer.
Such horizontal gene transfer produces a reticulate (non-tree-like DAG) biological history \citep{huson2005application}, which has been the focus of phylogenetic network literature defines several useful descriptors \citep{yu2011coalescent,solislemus2016inferring,choy2004computing,zhang2022weighting}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the number of reticulation nodes,
\item level, the extent to which reticulation cycles are isolated or overlap and interlock,
\item $\gamma$, the fractional size of each transfer (i.e., viewing distribution to distinguish few large transfers versus more frequent small transfers),
\item discordance among per-locus geneaologies (e.g., quartet distance) which reflects the cumulative fraction of genomes routed through horizontal transfers, as opposed to vertical edges.
\end{enumerate}
Useful software and tenchniques for layout and visualization of reticulate networks also exist \citep{huson2011phylogenetic,solislemus2017phylonetworks,huson2025sketch,panssen2024phylox,schliep2026tanggle}.
