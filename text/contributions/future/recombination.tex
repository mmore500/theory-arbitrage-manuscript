\subsection{Phylogenetic Networks}
\label{sec:recombination}

Mixture of genetic information between independent lineages is critical to efficient amplification of beneficial mutations and suppression of harmful ones in biology and evolutionary computation alike \citep{felsenstein1976evolutionary,holland1975adaptation}.
Unlike EC, however, such recombination is often highly asymmetric and irregular in biology.
Among microbes, in particular, widespread horizontal gene transfer (HGT) occurs by conjugation (plasmid exchange), transduction (virus-mediated exchange), and transformation (environmental uptake) \citep{arnold2022horizontal} --- carrying anything from individual genes to broad functional clusters \citep{brinkmann2018horizontal,martin2018physiological}.
Such HGT has extended regulatory networks \citep{price2008horizontal}, modified metabolic pathways \citep{goyal2022horizontal}, and promoted modularity \citep{kreimer2008evolution}.

While HGT may benefit the constructive power of evolution \citep{wiser2019horizontal}, it remains lightly studied in EC \citep{goldbarg2009transgeneic,peralesgravan2008am,harvey2011microbial,mehrafsa2013high,tomko2013unconstrain,atkinson2020horizontal}.
These existing studies primarily consider problem-solving outcomes, and do not actually report the extent of HGT incorporations within individual genomes or the structure of successful HGT events in broader evolutionary history.
Phylogenetic analysis of ancestry (Section \ref{sec:diversity-measures}) can help address these questions, but lineage reticulations introduced by recombination require extensions beyond purely tree-based approaches --- which assume lineages branch, but do not combine.

Instead of phylogenetic trees, such histories produce \textit{phylogenetic networks} --- which take the form of a directed acyclic graph (DAG) \citep{huson2005application,baroni2005framework}.
A wealth of useful visualization and analysis techniques, many with accompanying software, have developed around this class of data \citep{huson2011phylogenetic,solislemus2017phylonetworks,huson2025sketch,panssen2024phylox,schliep2026tanggle,than2008phylonet}.
In particular, phylogenetic network literature defines several useful metrics describing the frequency, size, structure, and impact of reticulations in evolutionary history \citep{zhang2022weighting}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textit{reticulation/hybridization number}, the number of reticulation events contributing to a lineage's history \citep{baroni2005bounding,baroni2006hybrids};
\item \textit{level}, the extent to which reticulation cycles are isolated or overlap and interlock \citep{choy2004computing};
\item \textit{inheritance probability} $\gamma$, the magnitude of individual transfers \citep{yu2011coalescent}; and
\item isolating a network's \textit{major tree} can distinguish backbone genetic content from lateral transfers \citep{solislemus2016inferring}.
\unskip\footnote{%
Other work presents a related concept of ``verticality'' \citep{nagies2020spectrum}.
}
\end{enumerate}

Reticulated phylogenetic networks can also be viewed through the lens of individual gene histories (i.e., \textit{gene trees}) \citep{maddison1997gene,avise1989gene,pamilo1988relationships}.
Discordance among the relatedness of individual genes (e.g., quartet distance) can be applied to characterize the influence of horizontal transfer on genome structure \citep{solislemus2016inferring,zhang2022weighting}.

Recombination via sexual crossover organizes ancestry into yet more densely reticulated pedigree structures.
Existing work with such histories in EC has allowed detailed examination of example sequences of events that can lead runs to discover successful solutions \citep{mcphee2016visualizing,mcphee2016using}.
A key practical challenge, however, arises from the data intensity of exhaustive tracking \citep{mcphee2016using}: compared to asexual ancestry trees, many fewer lineages leave no descendants --- and so much more data must be retained \citep{dolson2020interpreting}.

In biology, where such detailed records are typically unavailable, analysis often zooms out to examine more tree-like relationships among species or subpopulations.
Such abstractions, though, must account for confounding processes like hybridization and incomplete lineage sorting (ILS) that complicate tree representation.
The former introduces genuine reticulation through gene flow, while the latter only mimics it by chance (shared ancestral variation can link lineages as if genes had crossed between them \citep{tajima1983evolutionary}).

By contrast, other work, under the banner of \textit{Ancestral Recombination Graphs (ARGs)}, instead performs analyses over large collections of gene trees \citep{nielsen2024inference,lewanski2024era,griffiths1997ancestral}.
Specialized software and data structures have been developed to handle the intricate structure of these networks \citep{godindubois2024apoget,kelleher2019inferring,wong2024args,jeffery2026tskit}.
Among other topics of interest to EC, work with ARGs has developed techniques for inference of selection on individual alleles \citep{hejase2021deeplearning,rasmussen2014genome,vaughn2024fast,korfmann2024simultaneous,hejase2020summary}.

The genealogy is typically stored as a directed acyclic graph where vertices represent individuals and arcs represent hereditary relationships between them. The graph structure also stores relevant metadata such as fitness values or types of genetic operators that were applied and the coordinates of genotype changes. Dynamical aspects such as the evolution of diversity over time are then able to be correlated with various quantifiable properties of the genealogy graph.
