\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}

Evolutionary Computation (EC) applies population-based search methods to optimize candidate solutions with respect to desired criteria \citep{fogel1999evolutionary}.
\unskip\footnote{%
Common instances of EC include genetic programming \citep{koza1992genetic}, evolutionary programming \citep{fogel1966artificial}, genetic algorithms \citep{mitchell1996introduction}, evolutionary algorithms \citep{holland1975adaptation}, neuroevolution \citep{stanley2002evolving}, differential evolution \citep{storn1997differential}, and evolution strategies \citep{rechenberg1973evolutionsstrategie}.
}
Within the field, the underlying metaphor of biological evolution so pervades that difficulty can arise in discerning the point where bio-inspiration ends and algorithm engineering begins.
While some work does reach well outside the biological metaphor \citep{hansen2001completely,munteanu1999improving,miller2015cartesian}, core aspects of EC squarely inhabit a bio-inspired framing --- whether originally conceived as such, or subsequently reframed (Table \ref{tab:bioinspiration}).

This ubiquity lends significant weight to concern over underlying merit of the biological metaphor \citep{moore2023evolution}.
Proponents of bio-inspiration highlight biological metaphors as a source of new ideas, and more simply as a source for convenient, intuitive, self-consistent vocabulary \citep{sorensen2013metaheuristics,banzhaf2006artificial}.
Another draw of bio-inspiration is the charisma it lends EC \citep{lehman2020surprising}.
Complexity and emergent intelligence found in the natural world present an irresistable display of evolution's profound creative power; the prospect of capturing any fraction of such capability is fascinating.

Along these lines, natural precedent can help prove the ultimate feasibility of ambitious research objectives \citep{miikkulainen2021biological,banzhaf2006artificial}.

The charisma of bio-inspiration, however, has also presented preoccupying distractions \citep{moore2023evolution,sorensen2013metaheuristics,aranha2021metaphorbased,campelo2023lessons}.
Given the tendency for machine learning researchers to adhere to their preferred models \citep{domingos2012few}, there is risk for evolutionary approaches to devolve into a pursuit for their own sake --- rather than a means to an end \citep{woodward2016gp,yampolskiy2018why}.
At the extreme, egregiously shoddy bio-inspiration can amount to mere naturalistic fallacy and window dressing on recycled ideas \citep{wortmann2020does,sorensen2013metaheuristics,simon2011analytical,campelo2023lessons,camachovillaln2022exposing,tzanetos2020nature,lones2019mitigating}.

Skepticism of the evolutionary metaphor echoes a more fundamental concern that EC lacks a cohesive, rigorous theoretical framework with first-principles grounding \citep{worzel2003genetic}, an anxiety also lingering from historical contention between ``scruffy'' and ``neat'' research philosophies \citep[p.~16]{jones2008artificial, minsky1991logical}.
On a practical level, the generally  \textit{ad hoc} nature of EC has imposed real cost in numerous arbitrary configurable and tunable elements that hinder adoption \citep{oneil2010open}.

One proposed path forward is to de-emphasize the evolutionary metaphor, to deepen and diversify alternate first principles footing for EC \citep{moore2023evolution}.
While we agree that EC needs sounder theory, we propose the opposite --- that the field's challenges should \textit{also} be addressed by leaning deeper into the evolutionary metaphor, in a new direction.

Historically, EC researchers have primarily drawn on bio-inspiration as means to invent new problem-solving mechanisms \citep{banzhaf2006artificial,kumar2003biologically,mcphee2009developmental}.

We argue one step further should be taken: to gather inspiration from the science arising around biology, rather than just the biology itself.
Such inspiration can also help improve understanding of how and why EC algorithms work, not just improve their performance.

Crucial to its breadth, evolutionary biology interleaves closely with many allied fields, such as population genetics, developmental biology, molecular biology, ethology (animal behavior), and ecology.
Particularly notable is the subfield of experimental evolution, which instantiates evolution in action under controlled conditions \citep{kawecki2012experimental} --- much the same as EC.
In the case of so-called ``directed evolution'' experiments, evolutionary optimization may even be recruited to a desired objective \citep{cobb2013directed,carroll2014applying}.

One concern in translating biology to EC is substrate specificity.
While fundamental differences do exist, it is also the case that life on Earth already requires biological abstractions to stretch across broad and diverse domains.
Indeed, computational artificial life approaches, a close cousin of evolutionary computation, heavily exploit such generality \citep{cleland2013general,langton1989artificial,pennock2007models}. % ELD: Lots more astrobiology citations we could add here

In many cases, the larger practical concern for effective arbitrage is familiarity with biological literature.
Insofar as content applicable to EC does exist, harnessing it requires sufficient domain knowledge (1) to identify it and (2) to navigate subtleties in aligning appropriate correspondences with EC.
Given the vastness, context-dependence, and --- at times --- polyonymy of biological theory, interdisciplinary collaborations can be highly productive \citep{goodman2020evolution}.

Where sufficient effort is invested and necesary relationships developed, engaging biological knowledge can yield substantial value in EC \citep{hu2010evolvability,dolson2018ecological,paixao2015toward}.

While promising, borrowing from biology is no silver bullet.
Importantly, substantial gaps remain in the explanatory and predictive power of ecology and evolution \citep{houlahan2016priority,catford2022addressing,yates2018outstanding}, despite the maturity and competence of these fields \citep{lynch2025complexity}.
Not unlike in EC, such deficiencies --- arising from an overabundance of identifiable causal factors and the existence of exceptions to nearly every rule --- produce perennial consternation \citep{welch2016whats}.
Failures by those outside the field to recognize and build on existing work have also been highlighted \citep{lynch2025complexity,beer2024alife}.

Nonetheless, bioscience equivalencies seem likely to exist for much of what explanatory power is possible within EC.
Because EC algorithms host populations of discrete individuals with heritable traits, they will --- in a literal sense --- instantiate evolutionary processes \citep{pennock2007models}.
Therefore, theory generalizable across EC will inherently exhibit isomorphisms to aspects of biology and \textit{vice versa}.
One fundamental difference, however, arises in the capability for digital evolution to abstract away otherwise universal pillars of biology (e.g., environmental heterogeneity, phenotypic plasticity, fitness stochasticity, etc.) \citep{belew1996computation}.
As such, digital evolution work complements biology in certain areas \citep{dolson2021digital}, a point we return to in our concluding remarks.
Nonetheless, while reciprocal exchange should be expected, it seems unlikely for the smaller field of EC to rival the breadth and depth of biology without making the most of borrowing.

Under an expanded perspective, bio-inspiration can not just produce new EC techniques, but also help explain how and why they work.
We believe looking past biology to harness the science that has arisen around it will be key to advancing the rigor, transparency, and efficacy of evolutionary computation.
By highlighting steps in this direction --- both already and not yet realized --- we hope to catalyze continued progress toward these goals.
