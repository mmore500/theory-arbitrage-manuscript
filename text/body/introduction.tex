\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}

Evolutionary Computation (EC) applies population-based search methods to optimize candidate solutions with respect to desired criteria \citep{fogel1999evolutionary}.
\unskip\footnote{%
Common instances of EC include genetic programming \citep{koza1992genetic}, evolutionary programming \citep{fogel1966artificial}, genetic algorithms \citep{mitchell1996introduction}, evolutionary algorithms \citep{holland1975adaptation}, neuroevolution \citep{stanley2002evolving}, differential evolution \citep{storn1997differential}, and evolution strategies \citep{rechenberg1973evolutionsstrategie}.
}
The metaphor of biological evolution so deeply pervades the field of EC that difficulty can arise in discerning the point where bio-inspiration ends and algorithm engineering begins.
Although some work extends well outside the biological metaphor \citep{hansen2001completely,munteanu1999improving,miller2015cartesian}, core aspects of EC inhabit a bio-inspired framing --- whether originally conceived as such, or subsequently reframed (Table \ref{tab:bioinspiration}).

Indeed, the prevalence of bio-inspiration within EC has made the merit of biological metaphors an important point of contention.
In addition to new ideas, some proponents highlight biological metaphors simply as a source for convenient, intuitive, self-consistent vocabulary \citep{sorensen2013metaheuristics,banzhaf2006artificial}.
Another draw of bio-inspiration is the charisma it lends EC \citep{lehman2020surprising}.
Complexity and emergent intelligence found in the natural world display evolution's profound creative power; the prospect of capturing any fraction of such capability is fascinating.

Along these lines, natural precedent is also useful in demonstrating the feasibility of ambitious objectives \citep{miikkulainen2021biological,banzhaf2006artificial}.

Some have questioned, however, whether the charisma of the bio-inspiration might also create a preoccupying distraction \citep{moore2023evolution,sorensen2013metaheuristics,aranha2021metaphorbased,campelo2023lessons}.
Given the tendency for machine learning researchers to adhere to their preferred models \citep{domingos2012few}, there is risk that evolutionary approaches have become something of a pursuit for their own sake --- rather than a means to an end \citep{woodward2016gp,yampolskiy2018why}.
Furthmore, especially shoddy bio-inspiration can amount to mere window dressing on recycled ideas and naturalistic fallacy \citep{wortmann2020does,sorensen2013metaheuristics,simon2011analytical,campelo2023lessons,camachovillaln2022exposing,tzanetos2020nature,lones2019mitigating}.

Skepticism of the evolutionary metaphor echoes a more fundamental concern that EC lacks a cohesive, rigorous theoretical framework with first-principles grounding \citep{worzel2003genetic}, an anxiety also lingering from historical contention between ``scruffy'' and ``neat'' philosophies \citep[p.~16]{jones2008artificial, minsky1991logical}.
On a practical level, the \textit{ad hoc} nature of EC imposes real cost from numerous arbitrary configurable and tunable elements \citep{oneil2010open}.

One possible path forward is to deepen and diversifying the first principles footing of EC by de-emphasizing the evolutionary metaphor \citep{moore2023evolution}.
While we agree that EC needs sounder theory, we propose the opposite --- that the field's challenges should also be addressed by leaning \textit{deeper} into the evolutionary metaphor, but in a new direction.

Historically, EC researchers have primarily drawn on bio-inspiration as means to produce new problem-solving mechanisms \citep{banzhaf2006artificial,kumar2003biologically,mcphee2009developmental}.

We argue for one step further: to take inspiration from the science arising around biology, rather than just the biology itself.
Such inspiration can help improve understanding of how EC algorithms behave, not just improve their performance.

Given that EC operates on the same principles as biological evolution, it is not surprising for biological theory to pertain directly on evolution \textit{in silico} \citep{belew1996computation,pennock2007models}.
Crucially to its breadth, evolutionary biology interleaves closely with many allied fields, such as population genetics, developmental biology, molecular biology, ethology (animal behavior), and ecology.
Particularly notable is the subfield of experimental evolution, which instantiates evolution in action under controlled conditions \citep{kawecki2012experimental} --- much the same as EC.
In the case of so-called ``directed evolution'' experiments, evolutionary optimization may even be recruited to a selected-for objective \citep{cobb2013directed,carroll2014applying}.

One concern in translating biology to EC is substrate specificity.
While this is a valid concern, generalizing across life on Earth already requires biological abstractions to stretch across broad and diverse domains.
Indeed, computational artificial life approaches, a close cousin of evolutionary computation, rely heavily on such generalization \citep{cleland2013general,langton1989artificial,pennock2007models}. % ELD: Lots more astrobiology citations we could add here

In many cases, familiarity with biological literature presents the larger practical obstacle to effective arbitrage.
Insofar as content applicable to EC exists within biological literature, harnessing it requires sufficient domain knowledge (1) to identify it and (2) to navigate subtleties in aligning appropriate correspondences with EC.
Given the vastness, context-dependence, and --- at times --- polyonymy of biological theory, interdisciplinary collaborations can be highly productive \citep{goodman2020evolution}.

Though nontrivial, where effort is invested and relationships developed, engaging biological knowledge can yield substantial value \citep{hu2010evolvability,dolson2018ecological,paixao2015toward}.
As we will review, bioscience knowledge already been had substantial success in EC (Table \ref{tab:arbitrage-examples}).

While promising, borrowing from biology is no silver bullet.
Importantly, substantial gaps remain in the explanatory and predictive power of ecology and evolution \citep{houlahan2016priority,catford2022addressing,yates2018outstanding} --- despite the maturity and competence of these fields \citep{lynch2025complexity}.
Not unlike in EC, such deficiencies provide perennial consternation in evolutionary biology.
Challenges include an overabundance of identifiable causal factors and the existence of exceptions to nearly every generalization \citep{welch2016whats}.
Failures by those outside the field to recognize and build on existing work have also been highlighted \citep{lynch2025complexity,beer2024alife}.

Nonetheless, biological equivalencies seem likely to exist for much of what explanatory power is possible within EC.
Because EC algorithms instantiate populations of discrete individuals with heritable traits, they will, in some literal sense, host evolutionary processes \citep{pennock2007models}.
Therefore, theory generalizable across EC will likely pertain to aspects of biology and \textit{vice versa}.
One otable exception, though, arises in the unique capabilities of digital evolution to entirely strip out core mechanisms of biology (e.g., environmental heterogeneity, phenotypic plasticity, fitness stochasticity, etc.).
As such, theory established in-house for EC can meaningfully complement biology in certain areas, a point we return to in our concluding remarks.
Nonetheless, while reciporical exchange should be expected, it seems unlikely for the smaller field of EC to rival the breadth and depth of biology without borrowing.

With expanded perspective, the evolution metaphor in EC has value to offer not only in inventing new techniques, but also in understanding how and why they work.
Looking past biology to harness the science that has arisen around it will be key in advancing the rigor, transparency, and efficacy of evolutionary computation.
By highlighting steps in this direction --- both realized and suggested --- we hope to catalyze continued progress toward these goals.
